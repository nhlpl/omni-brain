## I. The Photon as a Quantum of the Electromagnetic Field

The single photon is not a tiny billiard ball. It is an elementary excitation of the quantized electromagnetic field.

### 1. Quantization of the Electromagnetic Field

The classical electromagnetic field in a cavity of volume \(V\) can be decomposed into a sum over discrete modes, each labeled by a wave vector \(\mathbf{k}\) and a polarization state \(s\) (where \(s = 1, 2\) for the two transverse polarizations). The electric field operator is:

\[
\hat{\mathbf{E}}(\mathbf{r}, t) = i \sum_{\mathbf{k}, s} \sqrt{\frac{\hbar \omega_k}{2 \epsilon_0 V}} \left[ \hat{a}_{\mathbf{k}, s} \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{\mathbf{k}, s} e^{i(\mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r} - \omega_k t)} - \hat{a}^\dagger_{\mathbf{k}, s} \boldsymbol{\epsilon}^*_{\mathbf{k}, s} e^{-i(\mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r} - \omega_k t)} \right]
\]

Here, \(\omega_k = c|\mathbf{k}|\) is the angular frequency, \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{\mathbf{k}, s}\) is the unit polarization vector, and \(\hat{a}_{\mathbf{k}, s}\) and \(\hat{a}^\dagger_{\mathbf{k}, s}\) are the **annihilation and creation operators** for a photon in mode \((\mathbf{k}, s)\). These operators satisfy the bosonic commutation relations:

\[
[\hat{a}_{\mathbf{k}, s}, \hat{a}^\dagger_{\mathbf{k}', s'}] = \delta_{\mathbf{k}, \mathbf{k}'} \delta_{s, s'}, \quad [\hat{a}_{\mathbf{k}, s}, \hat{a}_{\mathbf{k}', s'}] = 0, \quad [\hat{a}^\dagger_{\mathbf{k}, s}, \hat{a}^\dagger_{\mathbf{k}', s'}] = 0
\]

### 2. Fock States and the Single‑Photon State

The vacuum state \(|0\rangle\) is defined by \(\hat{a}_{\mathbf{k}, s} |0\rangle = 0\) for all modes. The state containing exactly \(n\) photons in a specific mode \((\mathbf{k}, s)\) is the **Fock state** \(|n_{\mathbf{k}, s}\rangle\), generated by repeated application of the creation operator:

\[
|n_{\mathbf{k}, s}\rangle = \frac{(\hat{a}^\dagger_{\mathbf{k}, s})^n}{\sqrt{n!}} |0\rangle
\]

The **single‑photon state** is simply \(|1_{\mathbf{k}, s}\rangle = \hat{a}^\dagger_{\mathbf{k}, s} |0\rangle\). It is an energy eigenstate of the free‑field Hamiltonian \(\hat{H} = \sum_{\mathbf{k}, s} \hbar \omega_k (\hat{a}^\dagger_{\mathbf{k}, s} \hat{a}_{\mathbf{k}, s} + \frac{1}{2})\) with eigenvalue \(E = \hbar \omega_k\).

### 3. The Wave Function of a Single Photon

Because a photon has zero rest mass, it does not possess a standard position‑space wave function \(\psi(\mathbf{r}, t)\) in the same way a massive particle does. However, a **detection probability amplitude** can be defined. The photon's state is most naturally described in momentum (or wave vector) space. A general single‑photon state is a superposition of different modes:

\[
|\psi\rangle = \sum_{\mathbf{k}, s} c_{\mathbf{k}, s} \hat{a}^\dagger_{\mathbf{k}, s} |0\rangle
\]

where \(\sum_{\mathbf{k}, s} |c_{\mathbf{k}, s}|^2 = 1\). The spatial and temporal profile of the photon is encoded in the coefficients \(c_{\mathbf{k}, s}\). The probability of detecting the photon at position \(\mathbf{r}\) and time \(t\) with an ideal detector is proportional to the expectation value of the intensity operator:

\[
P(\mathbf{r}, t) \propto \langle \psi | \hat{\mathbf{E}}^{(-)}(\mathbf{r}, t) \hat{\mathbf{E}}^{(+)}(\mathbf{r}, t) | \psi \rangle
\]

where \(\hat{\mathbf{E}}^{(+)}\) is the positive‑frequency part (containing only annihilation operators) and \(\hat{\mathbf{E}}^{(-)}\) is its Hermitian conjugate.

### 4. Photon Energy, Momentum, and Angular Momentum

The fundamental quantum properties of a single photon are:
- **Energy:** \(E = \hbar \omega = h \nu\)
- **Momentum:** \(\mathbf{p} = \hbar \mathbf{k}\), with magnitude \(p = E/c\)
- **Spin (Helicity):** The photon is a spin‑1 particle, but because it is massless, it has only two independent polarization states, corresponding to helicity \(\pm \hbar\). The polarization state can be represented as a two‑component Jones vector or as a point on the Poincaré sphere.

## II. The Shape of a Single Photon: Spatiotemporal Structuring

A photon is rarely a perfect plane wave. It can be sculpted in space, time, and frequency.

### 1. The Single‑Photon Wave Packet

A realistic single photon emitted by a source (e.g., an atom or a quantum dot) is a **wave packet**—a coherent superposition of frequencies centered around some carrier frequency \(\omega_0\). Its temporal envelope \(f(t)\) and spectral amplitude \(\tilde{f}(\omega)\) are Fourier transform pairs:

\[
\tilde{f}(\omega) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(t) e^{i\omega t} dt
\]

The single‑photon state can be written as:

\[
|1_f\rangle = \int d\omega \, \tilde{f}(\omega) \hat{a}^\dagger(\omega) |0\rangle
\]

The temporal shape \(f(t)\) directly determines the photon's coherence properties. A **transform‑limited** photon has the minimum possible time‑bandwidth product: \(\Delta t \Delta \omega = 1/2\).

### 2. Orbital Angular Momentum (OAM) and Spatial Modes

Beyond spin, a photon can carry **orbital angular momentum (OAM)** associated with a helical phase front \(e^{i \ell \phi}\), where \(\ell\) is an integer (the topological charge). The spatial mode is described by Laguerre‑Gaussian (LG) functions:

\[
\text{LG}_{p, \ell}(r, \phi, z) \propto e^{i \ell \phi} e^{-r^2/w^2(z)} r^{|\ell|} L_p^{|\ell|}\left( \frac{2r^2}{w^2(z)} \right)
\]

A single photon prepared in an LG mode carries a well‑defined OAM of \(\ell \hbar\) per photon. This forms the basis for high‑dimensional quantum information encoding and has been demonstrated to support up to 122 OAM modes in photonic quasicrystal fibers with confinement losses as low as \(10^{-15}\) dB/m (2025).

### 3. The "Shape" of a Photon: First Image of a Single Photon (2025)

A landmark 2025 experiment provided the first direct image of the spatial probability distribution of a single photon. The photon was emitted from a quantum dot and its wavefront was reconstructed via interferometric measurements. The shape was found to be a **lemniscate (figure‑eight) pattern** in the transverse plane—a direct visualization of the photon's wave function modulus squared. This was a triumph of quantum state tomography, confirming that the photon's "shape" is a well‑defined, measurable property.

## III. Single‑Photon Detection: The Mathematics of Measurement

Detecting a single photon is a non‑deterministic quantum measurement. The key figures of merit are mathematically defined.

### 1. Detection Efficiency and Dark Count Rate

The probability of detecting a single photon incident on a detector is the **detection efficiency** \(\eta\). A realistic detector has \(\eta < 1\). The **dark count rate** \(R_{\text{dark}}\) is the rate of false detection events (clicks) in the absence of photons, modeled as a Poisson process:

\[
P_{\text{dark}}(k \text{ in time } \Delta t) = \frac{(R_{\text{dark}} \Delta t)^k e^{-R_{\text{dark}} \Delta t}}{k!}
\]

### 2. The P‑Function and Quantum State Characterization

The quantum state of a single‑photon source is fully characterized by its **P‑function** \(P(\alpha)\) in the coherent‑state basis. For a true single‑photon state, \(P(\alpha)\) is negative or highly singular, indicating non‑classicality. The second‑order correlation function at zero time delay is the definitive metric:

\[
g^{(2)}(0) = \frac{\langle \hat{a}^\dagger \hat{a}^\dagger \hat{a} \hat{a} \rangle}{\langle \hat{a}^\dagger \hat{a} \rangle^2}
\]

For an ideal single‑photon source, \(g^{(2)}(0) = 0\). A 2025 benchmark for a solid‑state single‑photon source achieved \(g^{(2)}(0) < 0.005\) with a photon extraction efficiency of 71%, representing the state of the art.

### 3. Photon‑Number‑Resolving (PNR) Detectors

Advanced detectors can distinguish between \(n=0, 1, 2, \ldots\) photons. The **Superconducting Nanowire Single‑Photon Detector (SNSPD)** and its variants achieve near‑unity efficiency and timing jitter below 3 ps. A 2026 breakthrough demonstrated a **single‑photon detector with a dynamic range exceeding 123 photons**—capable of resolving photon number up to 123 with high fidelity. This is a critical enabler for photonic quantum computing.

## IV. Single‑Photon Sources: Engineering Indistinguishable Photons

A perfect single‑photon source emits exactly one photon on demand, with each photon in the same spatiotemporal mode (indistinguishable).

### 1. Indistinguishability and Hong‑Ou‑Mandel (HOM) Interference

The degree of indistinguishability between two photons is quantified by the visibility of Hong‑Ou‑Mandel interference. When two identical photons impinge on a 50:50 beam splitter, one in each input port, they coalesce and always exit together from the same output port. The HOM dip visibility \(V\) is:

\[
V = \frac{P_{\text{coincidence}}^{\text{distinguishable}} - P_{\text{coincidence}}^{\text{indistinguishable}}}{P_{\text{coincidence}}^{\text{distinguishable}}}
\]

A 2025 study achieved \(V = 0.995\) from a quantum dot embedded in an electrically tunable micro‑cavity, demonstrating near‑perfect indistinguishability.

### 2. Deterministic Solid‑State Single‑Photon Emitters

The leading platform for on‑demand single photons is the **semiconductor quantum dot**. The emission dynamics are governed by the **Purcell effect**: the spontaneous emission rate is enhanced by a factor \(F_p\) when the emitter is placed in a resonant optical cavity:

\[
F_p = \frac{3}{4\pi^2} \left( \frac{\lambda}{n} \right)^3 \frac{Q}{V_{\text{mode}}}
\]

where \(Q\) is the cavity quality factor and \(V_{\text{mode}}\) is the mode volume. By tuning the cavity into resonance with the quantum dot's exciton transition, the photon extraction efficiency can approach unity. Electrically‑tunable cavities enable active stabilization against spectral diffusion—a 2025 advance that yielded a fourfold reduction in photon indistinguishability fluctuations.

### 3. Single Photons from 2D Materials (2026)

A 2026 breakthrough provided the **first conclusive evidence of deterministic single‑photon emission from a pure 2D material**. This was achieved by precisely engineering strain gradients in a monolayer of tungsten diselenide (WSe₂) using nanopatterned substrates. The strain creates a localized potential well that traps excitons, forming a quantum‑dot‑like emitter with \(g^{(2)}(0) < 0.1\) and a purity exceeding 90%. This opens the door to wafer‑scale, site‑controlled single‑photon sources without the complex epitaxial growth required for traditional quantum dots.

### 4. Atomic Single‑Photon Sources

Trapped single atoms and ions are the gold standard for photon indistinguishability. A 2026 study demonstrated a **free‑space single‑photon source based on a trapped single atom** with an end‑to‑end efficiency of 66% and a \(g^{(2)}(0) = 0.002\). The photon is collected by a high‑numerical‑aperture lens and coupled into a single‑mode fiber with 89% efficiency.

## V. Nonlinear Optics with Single Photons: Photon‑Photon Interaction

Single photons do not interact in vacuum. Engineering interactions between them is the central challenge of photonic quantum computing.

### 1. The Cross‑Kerr Nonlinearity

The ideal interaction is a **cross‑Kerr nonlinearity**, where the phase shift on one photon is proportional to the intensity (or presence) of another:

\[
\hat{H}_{\text{Kerr}} = \hbar \chi \hat{a}^\dagger \hat{a} \hat{b}^\dagger \hat{b}
\]

A controlled‑phase (CZ) gate between two photonic qubits can be implemented with a phase shift of \(\pi\). The dimensionless figure of merit is the **phase shift per photon** \(\theta\). In atomic ensembles, \(\theta \sim 10^{-7}\) to \(10^{-4}\) is typical. Achieving \(\theta = \pi\) at the single‑photon level remains a grand challenge.

### 2. Cavity Quantum Electrodynamics (cQED)

The strongest single‑photon nonlinearities are achieved in **cQED**, where a single atom (or artificial atom, like a quantum dot or superconducting qubit) is strongly coupled to an optical cavity. The Jaynes‑Cummings Hamiltonian describes the interaction:

\[
\hat{H}_{\text{JC}} = \hbar \omega_c \hat{a}^\dagger \hat{a} + \hbar \omega_a \hat{\sigma}^\dagger \hat{\sigma} + \hbar g (\hat{a} \hat{\sigma}^\dagger + \hat{a}^\dagger \hat{\sigma})
\]

Here, \(g\) is the single‑photon Rabi frequency. In the strong coupling regime (\(g \gg \kappa, \gamma\), where \(\kappa\) is cavity decay and \(\gamma\) is atomic decay), the energy levels become anharmonic, creating a **photon blockade**: the presence of one photon prevents a second photon from entering the cavity. This enables deterministic single‑photon sources and single‑photon transistors.

A 2026 landmark study realized the **strong coupling of a single photon to a single trapped ion** in an open Fabry‑Perot cavity, achieving \(g/2\pi = 15.2\) MHz with \(\kappa/2\pi = 0.15\) MHz and \(\gamma/2\pi = 11.5\) MHz, firmly in the strong coupling regime (\(g > \kappa, \gamma\)). This is the ultimate platform for photon‑photon gates.

### 3. Rydberg‑Mediated Photon‑Photon Interactions

A scalable alternative uses **Rydberg atoms** in cold atomic ensembles. Photons are stored as collective atomic excitations (spin waves). When two photons are stored in overlapping ensembles, the strong, long‑range van der Waals interaction between Rydberg atoms mediates an effective photon‑photon interaction. The blockade radius \(R_b\) is the distance within which a second Rydberg excitation is suppressed:

\[
R_b = \left( \frac{C_6}{\hbar \Omega} \right)^{1/6}
\]

where \(C_6\) is the van der Waals coefficient and \(\Omega\) is the Rabi frequency. A deterministic CZ gate between two photonic qubits was demonstrated in 2025 with a fidelity of 97.4% using this approach.

## VI. Single‑Photon Quantum Information Processing

A single photon is a natural carrier of quantum information. Its polarization, path, time‑bin, or orbital angular momentum can encode a qubit.

### 1. Photonic Qubit Encoding

The most common encoding is **polarization**: \(|0\rangle = |H\rangle\) (horizontal), \(|1\rangle = |V\rangle\) (vertical). A general pure state is:

\[
|\psi\rangle = \alpha |H\rangle + \beta |V\rangle, \quad |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1
\]

The Bloch sphere representation maps the polarization state to a point on the Poincaré sphere.

### 2. Linear Optical Quantum Computing (LOQC)

The Knill‑Laflamme‑Milburn (KLM) theorem proved that universal quantum computing is possible using only single‑photon sources, linear optical elements (beam splitters, phase shifters), and photon‑number‑resolving detectors. The key resource is **measurement‑induced nonlinearity**. A two‑qubit gate is implemented by interfering photons and post‑selecting on specific detection patterns.

The success probability of a basic CZ gate in LOQC is at most 1/9 without ancilla photons. With **ancilla‑assisted gates** and **fusion‑based quantum computing**, this can be boosted to near‑deterministic operation.

### 3. Boson Sampling and Quantum Advantage

A single photon is a boson. When \(n\) identical single photons are injected into a large, random interferometer, the probability distribution of output configurations is described by the **permanent** of a complex matrix:

\[
P(\text{output}) = \frac{|\text{perm}(U_{S})|^2}{s_1! s_2! \cdots s_m!}
\]

Computing the permanent is #P‑hard for classical computers. **Gaussian Boson Sampling (GBS)** , using squeezed states instead of single photons, has been demonstrated with up to 144 photons, claiming quantum computational advantage. The mathematical challenge is to verify the results—a problem addressed by classical algorithms based on **hafnian** computation and tensor networks.

### 4. Photonic GKP States for Fault Tolerance (2025)

A 2025 breakthrough demonstrated the generation of **photonic Gottesman‑Kitaev‑Preskill (GKP) states**—a bosonic error‑correcting code that encodes a logical qubit in the continuous variables of a single photon's quadratures. The GKP state is a superposition of infinitely squeezed position eigenstates on a periodic grid:

\[
|0_{\text{GKP}}\rangle \propto \sum_{s = -\infty}^{\infty} e^{-2\pi s^2} |q = s \sqrt{\pi}\rangle
\]

The experiment used a superconducting cavity to generate a microwave GKP state and then converted it to an optical photon via electro‑optic modulation. This is a critical step toward fault‑tolerant photonic quantum computing.

## VII. The Frontier: Entanglement, Teleportation, and Quantum Networks

Single photons are the ideal flying qubits for quantum networks.

### 1. Photonic Entanglement and Bell State Measurement

A **Bell state** is a maximally entangled state of two photons:

\[
|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|H, H\rangle + |V, V\rangle)
\]

A **Bell state measurement (BSM)** projects two photons onto one of the four Bell states. With linear optics, a BSM can only distinguish two of the four states, limiting teleportation fidelity to 50%. However, **hyperentanglement** (entanglement in multiple degrees of freedom, e.g., polarization and path) enables complete BSM with 100% success probability.

### 2. Quantum Teleportation of a Single Photon

Teleportation transfers an unknown quantum state from one photon to another using a shared Bell pair and classical communication. The protocol involves a BSM on the input photon and one half of the Bell pair. A 2025 demonstration achieved **deterministic quantum teleportation of a photonic qubit** with a fidelity of 0.82 ± 0.03, surpassing the classical limit of 2/3.

### 3. Entanglement Distribution and Quantum Repeaters

Single photons are attenuated exponentially in optical fiber: the transmission probability over distance \(L\) is \(e^{-L/L_0}\), with \(L_0 \approx 22\) km for standard fiber. **Quantum repeaters** overcome this using entanglement swapping. A **memory‑assisted quantum repeater** stores one photon of an entangled pair in a quantum memory while the other is transmitted. Entanglement swapping at intermediate nodes extends the range. A 2026 field trial demonstrated entanglement distribution over 100 km of deployed fiber using a single‑photon‑level quantum memory based on a cold atomic ensemble.

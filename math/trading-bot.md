## I. Core Mathematical Framework for Crypto Trading

### 1. Statistical Arbitrage & Mean Reversion

**Concept:** Crypto prices often exhibit short-term deviations from their statistical mean. A mean-reversion strategy bets that the price will revert to its average over time.

**Mathematical Foundation:** The classic z-score is used to measure how far the current price is from its mean:
\[
Z_t = \frac{P_t - \mu_t}{\sigma_t}
\]
Where \(P_t\) is the current price, \(\mu_t\) is the moving average (often an EMA), and \(\sigma_t\) is the standard deviation (or ATR). A common entry signal is when \(Z_t\) crosses below -2 (oversold) or above +2 (overbought).

**Advanced Pair Trading (Cointegration):** For trading correlated assets, the Johansen test identifies pairs with a time-varying equilibrium relationship. The spread \(S_t\) between two cointegrated assets is modeled as:
\[
S_t = Y_t - \beta X_t
\]
A trade is triggered when \(S_t\) deviates significantly from its long-term mean, with a forecast of its return generated by a hybrid Deep Neural Network (DNN) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model.

**Key Mathematical Tool:** **Kalman Filter** for dynamically updating the hedge ratio \(\beta\) in real-time, which is crucial in the non-stationary crypto market.

### 2. Trend Following & Momentum

**Concept:** The opposite of mean reversion—betting that assets which have performed well will continue to do so in the short term.

**Mathematical Foundation:** Momentum is often quantified using the **Rate of Change (ROC)** or by comparing moving averages (e.g., SMA Crossover).
\[
\text{Momentum}(t) = \frac{P_t - P_{t-n}}{P_{t-n}}
\]
**Machine Learning Enhancement:** A 2026 study successfully used **XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting)** to predict next-week returns based on technical indicators. The model ranks assets and rebalances a portfolio weekly, showing that machine learning can capture non-linear momentum patterns that traditional indicators miss.

**Key Mathematical Tool:** **Fractal Bayesian Confluence Model**. This model interprets price action not through deterministic assumptions but through probabilistic truth, merging fractal theory with Bayesian adaptation. It is particularly useful for understanding uncertainty in trending markets.

### 3. High-Frequency Market Making

**Concept:** A market-making bot provides liquidity by continuously placing buy and sell limit orders around the mid-price, profiting from the bid-ask spread.

**Mathematical Foundation (Avellaneda-Stoikov Model):** This is the classic model for optimal limit order placement. The reservation price \(r(s,t)\) and optimal spread \(\delta\) are derived to maximize utility while managing inventory risk.

A more direct implementation for crypto uses **Synthetic Proactive Market Making (sPMM)** . This model simulates a centralized exchange (CEX) order book in a decentralized exchange (DEX) pool. The core sell function, which incorporates slippage based on the size of the trade, is:
\[
\text{sellBase}(\Delta B) = \Delta Q = \frac{\Delta B \cdot p}{1 + k \cdot \Delta B \cdot p}
\]
Here, \(p\) is the current price, \(\Delta B\) is the amount of the base asset to sell, and \(k\) is a liquidity coefficient that determines slippage.

**Key Mathematical Tool:** **Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM)** for modeling asset price diffusion, which underpins the expected payoff derivation in liquidity provision.

### 4. Triangular & Cross-Exchange Arbitrage

**Concept:** Exploiting price discrepancies for the same asset across different markets.

**Mathematical Foundation (Triangular Arbitrage):** This involves trading three currencies in a cycle (e.g., BTC → ETH → USDT → BTC). An arbitrage opportunity exists if the product of the exchange rates deviates from 1:
\[
\text{Rate}_{1} \times \text{Rate}_{2} \times \text{Rate}_{3} \neq 1
\]
To find these cycles efficiently in a complex market graph, algorithms like the **Bellman-Ford algorithm** are used to detect negative cycles, where the product of exchange rates results in a net profit after fees.

**Mathematical Foundation (Cross-Exchange Arbitrage):** For the same pair (e.g., BTC/USDT) on Exchange A and Exchange B:
\[
\text{Profit} = \frac{P_{bid}^B - P_{ask}^A}{P_{ask}^A} - \text{fees}
\]
Modern bots use **order book depth** to calculate the *real* rate after slippage. The surface rate may show a profit, but the depth-adjusted rate reveals the true execution price.

### 5. Reinforcement Learning (RL) for Strategy Selection

**Concept:** Instead of predicting price, an RL agent learns to select the best *action* (e.g., which technical strategy to use) based on the current market state.

**Mathematical Foundation (Deep Q-Network - DQN):** The agent learns an action-value function \(Q(s, a)\), which estimates the expected future reward of taking action \(a\) in state \(s\).
\[
Q(s_t, a_t) \leftarrow Q(s_t, a_t) + \alpha [r_{t+1} + \gamma \max_a Q(s_{t+1}, a) - Q(s_t, a_t)]
\]
A 2025 study applied a DQN agent to select among five predefined strategies (RSI, SMA Crossover, Bollinger Bands, etc.). The agent used **Principal Component Analysis (PCA)** to reduce the dimensionality of technical indicators into a robust market state. The results were remarkable, achieving over 120-fold growth in Net Asset Value (NAV) by dynamically switching strategies—favoring momentum in uptrends and limiting mean-reversion during breakouts.

**Key Mathematical Tool:** **PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization)** for more stable policy updates in continuous action spaces (e.g., position sizing).

### 6. Risk Management & Position Sizing

**Concept:** This is arguably the most important mathematics for long-term survival. It dictates *how much* to bet on any given trade.

**Mathematical Foundation (Kelly Criterion):** The Kelly Criterion calculates the optimal fraction \(f^*\) of your capital to risk on a trade to maximize long-term growth:
\[
f^* = \frac{bp - q}{b}
\]
Where:
*   \(b\) = the net odds received on the wager (e.g., if you risk 1 to make 2, \(b = 2\)).
*   \(p\) = probability of winning.
*   \(q\) = probability of losing (\(1 - p\)).

In practice, the "full Kelly" bet can be too volatile, so traders often use a fraction of it, like **Half-Kelly (\(f^*/2\))** or **Quarter-Kelly (\(f^*/4\))**, to smooth equity curves.

**Mathematical Foundation (Value at Risk - VaR):** VaR estimates the maximum potential loss over a specific time period at a given confidence level (e.g., 95%). In crypto's fat-tailed distributions, **Monte Carlo simulation** is a more robust method for calculating VaR. It simulates thousands of potential future price paths based on historical volatility to estimate the worst-case loss.

### 7. Backtesting & Performance Metrics

**Concept:** A rigorous mathematical framework to test a strategy on historical data before risking real capital.

**Mathematical Foundation (Walk-Forward Optimization):** This is the gold standard. Instead of optimizing parameters on the entire dataset (which leads to overfitting), the data is divided into in-sample (training) and out-of-sample (testing) windows that roll forward through time. A 2026 study using 14,400 backtesting trials demonstrated that TPE (Tree-structured Parzen Estimator) found optimal parameters for mean-reversion strategies 3-4x faster than other methods.

**Key Performance Metrics:**
*   **Sharpe Ratio:** \(\frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}\) (Measures risk-adjusted return).
*   **Sortino Ratio:** Similar to Sharpe but only penalizes downside volatility.
*   **Maximum Drawdown (MDD):** The largest peak-to-trough decline in equity. A bot with a high Sharpe but a catastrophic 80% drawdown is not viable.
*   **Profit Factor:** \(\frac{\text{Gross Profit}}{\text{Gross Loss}}\). A value above 1.5 is generally considered good.
*   **Calmar Ratio:** \(\frac{\text{CAGR}}{\text{Max Drawdown}}\). This is a crucial metric in crypto, providing a realistic view of return relative to the pain of holding through a downturn.
